The Petra Water System

Nabataean · c. 300 BCE – 300 CE

Petra sits in a desert basin that receives almost no reliable rainfall — yet it sustained a city of roughly 30,000 people. The Nabataeans solved this with a distribution network, not a single structure: hillside catchment channels fed sandstone cisterns sealed with a waterproof gypsum-lime plaster, diversion dams pulled destructive flash floods away from the city center and into storage, and rock-cut or terracotta pipelines carried water for kilometers into the urban core.

The detail that reads as pure engineering: pipeline gradients were deliberately matched to each spring's natural output, so the system moved exactly as much water as the source could sustainably give — fast enough to stay usable, slow enough to avoid erosion and leakage. No pumps, no external energy input. Just topography, read correctly.

Qanat Fir'aun — The Gadara Aqueduct

Roman · built 90–210 CE

A 170-kilometer supply line built to bring water from reservoirs in modern-day Syria to Gadara, a Decapolis city of about 50,000 people — the longest underground tunnel built anywhere in antiquity, roughly 106 km of it cut through solid rock.

The tunnel was excavated using qanat technique: thousands of vertical shafts sunk at intervals to reach the planned tunnel depth, with crews then cutting horizontally in both directions from adjacent shafts until the sections met. Surveying had to hold to gradients under one part per thousand across tens of kilometers — with no laser levels, no GPS, and construction crews who couldn't see each other underground. It's a tolerance problem that would be taken seriously on a modern tunnel-boring project.

Engineering Notebook
  • Total length170 km (≈106 km underground)
  • Build duration~120 years, in stages
  • Typical slope< 1 ‰ over long sections
  • Core challengeIndependent survey alignment, two-way excavation
32.2809°N, 35.8996°E — Jerash (ancient Gerasa)

The Water-Powered Stone Saw, Temple of Artemis

Byzantine · 6th century CE

By the sixth century, the disused Temple of Artemis had become a quarry workshop. Excavators found a mill race built to channel water onto a wheel, and — more strikingly — limestone column drums scored with evenly spaced, dead-straight saw marks: the signature of a mechanized cut, not hand sawing.

The reconstructed drive train is a crank-and-connecting-rod mechanism — a slider-crank linkage — turning the wheel's continuous rotation into the back-and-forth stroke a saw blade needs. It's the same fundamental linkage in a kinematics course today, found here — and at sister sites in Ephesus and Hierapolis — centuries before this class of mechanism was long assumed to have first appeared in medieval Europe.

Why this exists

An undergraduate's field notebook, built into an archive.

This project started from a simple frustration: Jordan's engineering history is usually told as archaeology or tourism, rarely as engineering — as a set of design decisions someone had to get right, under real constraints, with the tools of their century.

It's built and maintained by a mechanical engineering undergraduate at Mutah University, as a long-term catalog rather than a finished product. Each site is treated as a case study: what problem it solved, what the mechanism or system actually was, and what a modern engineer would recognize in it.
